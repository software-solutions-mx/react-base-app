**Document type:** Designer Handoff — Logo & Visual Identity Only

**Slogan:** Supporting Life with Truth, Hope, and Care — Because Every Life Deserves a Voice.
**Audience:** AI image generation agent / AI design agent

**Not for:** Developers or coders — this is a pure design brief

---

## ⚠️ Before You Begin — Critical Context

This organization supports women facing unexpected pregnancies. Every visual asset you create must communicate **warmth, safety, hope, and trust**. The person seeing these visuals may be frightened, alone, and in a moment of crisis. Your designs must make her feel: _"I am safe here. Someone is speaking for me."_

The name **Voices Bringing Hope to Life** carries three powerful visual metaphors that must guide all design decisions:

- **Voices** — sound, expression, breath, resonance, being heard, advocacy
- **Hope** — light, warmth, upward movement, dawn, new beginning, an open future
- **Life** — growth, nature, breath, the living world, a seedling, a leaf, a heartbeat

The slogan **"Because Every Life Deserves a Voice"** adds a fourth layer: **dignity** — the idea that something small and new deserves to be spoken for, protected, championed. This is the emotional soul of the brand.

Every mark you design should quietly carry all four of these ideas — Voices, Hope, Life, Dignity — without being literal or heavy-handed about any of them.

## Part 1 — Brand Identity Foundation

### Personality in Five Words

**Compassionate. Credible. Hopeful. Protective. Human.**

### What Every Visual Must Feel Like

- A trusted companion speaking gently in a difficult moment
- An open door, not a closed fist
- Quiet confidence, not a shout
- Warm and safe, not clinical or alarming
- Hopeful, not heavy

### What No Visual May Ever Feel Like

- Political, combative, or campaign-like
- Religious in an exclusive or dogmatic way (default to non-denominational)
- Clinical, medical, or procedural
- Sentimental in a manipulative or exploitative way
- Alarming, urgent, or fear-based
- Cheap, generic, or amateurish
- Corporate or cold

## Part 3 — Typography for the Wordmark

### Organization Name Typesetting

The full name **Voices Bringing Hope to Life** is long. It must be handled thoughtfully across logo versions.

**Primary wordmark font:** Merriweather Bold (weight 700)

- Source: Google Fonts — free
- Style: Warm, editorial serif — conveys trust, seriousness, and compassion simultaneously
- Letter-spacing: `-0.02em` (slightly tighter than default)
- Do not distort, stretch, or italicize the letterforms

**Recommended typesetting approaches for the horizontal logo:**

Option A — Single line, all words:

```
Voices Bringing Hope to Life
```

Set in Merriweather Bold. The word **"Voices"** may optionally be set in Deep Life Blue (`#2E5B8A`) with the remaining words in Charcoal Navy (`#26313C`) to create visual hierarchy and draw the eye to the identity anchor.

Option B — Two-line stacked wordmark:

```
Voices Bringing
Hope to Life
```

Line 1 slightly smaller (approximately 85% of line 2 cap height). Line 2 in full weight. Both lines left-aligned.

Option C — Three-line editorial stack:

```
      VOICES
Bringing Hope
  to Life
```

"VOICES" in uppercase, tracked wide (+0.12em), slightly smaller. "Bringing Hope" in full Merriweather Bold. "to Life" in Merriweather Regular italic, slightly smaller. This option has the most character and editorial personality.

**Recommended:** Option B for the horizontal logo (cleanest at small sizes). Option C for the stacked/centered logo (most personality at larger display sizes).

### Slogan / Tagline Typesetting

**Full slogan:** _Supporting Life with Truth, Hope, and Care — Because Every Life Deserves a Voice._

This slogan is too long for logo use. Use the **short form** in logo applications:

**Short-form tagline for logo use:**

> _Because Every Life Deserves a Voice._

**Font:** Inter Regular (weight 400) or Inter Medium (weight 500)
**Size relative to wordmark:** 30–38% of wordmark cap height
**Color:** `#5A6E74` (Slate/Stone — muted, not competing with wordmark)
**Letter-spacing:** `+0.03em`
**Style:** Sentence case, not all-caps

The tagline appears only in the **primary horizontal logo** and **stacked logo** versions where there is sufficient space. It does not appear in icon-only, wordmark-only, or reversed small-scale versions.

### Typography Prohibition Rules

- Never use any font other than Merriweather Bold for the organization name in the wordmark
- Never use any font other than Inter for the tagline
- Never place the wordmark on a background where text contrast falls below 4.5:1
- Never outline the wordmark letterforms
- Never use decorative, script, or novelty fonts anywhere in the logo system

## Part 4 — The Logo Mark (Symbol)

### Conceptual Brief

The name **Voices Bringing Hope to Life** gives the designer rich visual material. The mark must distill the essence of this name without being literal. A voice is breath, sound, resonance. Hope is light, warmth, dawn. Life is growth, nature, emergence. Together they suggest: _something small and fragile finding its voice and rising toward the light._

The mark should carry this feeling in the simplest possible form — three to four visual elements maximum, readable at 32×32px, beautiful at 512×512px.

---

### Concept A — The Leaf with Voice Lines _(Primary Recommended Concept)_

**The idea:**
A single, abstracted leaf form — representing Life and growth — from which two or three gentle curved lines radiate upward and outward from its center, like sound waves, breath, or a voice speaking. The leaf is the life. The radiating lines are the voice. Together they become: a voice bringing hope to life.

**Geometry and construction:**

_The leaf base:_

- A single, softly asymmetric leaf silhouette — not a botanical illustration, an abstracted form
- Wider at the lower third, tapering to a soft point at the top
- The left edge is slightly more curved than the right, giving the form gentle organic life
- A single midrib runs from the rounded base up to the tip — same color as the leaf, slightly reduced opacity (65%)
- The overall leaf fits within a vertical rectangle, approximately 1:1.7 ratio
- No visible stem — the leaf begins cleanly at its own base

_The voice lines:_

- Two or three smooth, arcing curves that emanate from the upper-right area of the leaf — as if rising from its heart
- Each arc is a clean, single-weight stroke (1.5–2pt equivalent) — not arrows, not sound bars, not Wi-Fi symbols. Organic curves, not concentric semicircles
- The arcs are unequal in length and curvature — the shortest is tightest and closest to the leaf, the longest sweeps widest and highest. This unevenness is intentional and gives warmth
- The arcs curve gently upward and to the right, as if sound is rising toward light
- The arcs do not connect to the leaf outline — they float just beyond it, beginning approximately 4pt from the leaf edge
- Stroke weight: consistent at 1.5pt, tapering to 0.75pt at the ends (not mechanical — gently calligraphic)

_Together:_

- The leaf and voice lines combine into a single mark that reads as unified, not as two separate elements
- The negative space between the leaf and the arcs is intentional — it gives the mark breath
- The overall mark is slightly asymmetric, skewed toward upper-right energy (voice rising, hope ascending)
- Fits within a square bounding box with the leaf occupying the lower-left two-thirds and the voice arcs in the upper-right third

**Color application:**

Primary version:

- Leaf: solid fill, Deep Life Blue (`#2E5B8A`)
- Midrib: same blue at 65% opacity
- Voice arcs: Deep Life Blue (`#2E5B8A`) — same as leaf for unity

Warm accent version (optional second colorway):

- Leaf: Deep Life Blue (`#2E5B8A`)
- Voice arcs: Warm Apricot (`#F4A261`) — the voice carries warmth and hope

Reversed version:

- Leaf and arcs: both white (`#FFFFFF`) on Deep Navy (`#1E3D5C`) background

**Why this works:**
The name "Voices Bringing Hope to Life" is directly encoded in the mark — the leaf is Life, the arcs are Voices, and the upward movement carries Hope. It does this without being literal or heavy-handed. It reads as a gentle, nature-based organizational symbol. It works at all sizes. It has personality without being trendy.

---

### Concept B — The Rising Seed with Breath

**The idea:**
A small seed or sprout at the base — representing new Life beginning — from which a single elegant stem rises, and from the stem's tip, two small leaves unfurl while a single smooth arc curves above them, like a protective voice speaking over the life below.

**Geometry:**

_The seed/sprout:_

- A small, rounded oval form at the base — the seed. This is the beginning point.
- From the seed, a single gently curved stem rises — not perfectly vertical, with a subtle leftward curve that resolves to center at the top
- The stem has slight taper — heavier at base, lighter at tip
- Two small paired leaves emerge from the upper stem — left leaf slightly larger, both unfurling upward and outward. Teardrop forms with blunt bases and soft pointed tips.

_The voice arc:_

- A single large, smooth arc curves above and around the sprout — wider than the sprout, forming a loose, open protective curve above it. Like a voice reaching over to shelter what is growing.
- The arc does not close into a full circle — it is open at the base, suggesting possibility rather than containment
- The arc's stroke tapers at both ends, heavier in the middle

_Together:_

- The arc above the sprout reads as protective — a voice speaking over new life
- The overall composition is vertical, approximately 1:1.4 ratio
- The seed at bottom, leaves at middle, arc above — a clear visual narrative of life, growth, and voice

**Color application:**

- Stem and leaves: Deep Life Blue (`#2E5B8A`)
- Protective arc: Deep Life Blue (`#2E5B8A`) — unified; or optionally Warm Apricot (`#F4A261`) for the arc only, making the "voice" element warm and hope-colored

---

### Concept C — The Sound Wave as Landscape

**The idea:**
Three or four gently undulating horizontal lines — like sound waves — that, when viewed together, also suggest rolling hills, a natural horizon, or the gentle curve of a leaf. They are simultaneously a voice and a landscape — a living world being spoken into existence.

**Geometry:**

- Three parallel flowing curves, stacked vertically with equal spacing
- Each curve has a gentle, organic undulation — not mechanical sine waves, not straight lines. Like a hand-drawn horizon.
- The curves are slightly different in their wave shape — no two are identical — giving the mark handmade warmth
- The lowest curve is the widest and most gently undulating (like the earth)
- The middle curve has a slightly more pronounced central rise (like a hill or a leaf spine)
- The uppermost curve is the narrowest and most graceful (like a voice rising)
- The overall form fits within a horizontal rectangle, approximately 2.5:1 ratio

_Optional enhancement:_

- A single small leaf or seed form appears within the negative space of the middle curve — half-hidden, as if being protected by the waves/voice around it. This ties the "Life" element in without adding another separate component.

**Color application:**

- All three curves: Deep Life Blue (`#2E5B8A`)
- Optional inner leaf: Warm Apricot (`#F4A261`) at reduced opacity (40%) — a quiet presence

**Why this works:**
This concept is the most abstract and the most sophisticated. It works beautifully at large scale on the website hero and at small scale as a favicon. It avoids all literal medical/political imagery while elegantly encoding Voices (sound waves), Hope (rising), and Life (landscape/leaf).

---

### Concept D — The Open Hand Holding a Leaf

**The idea:**
The most emotionally direct of the four concepts. A single open, upward-facing hand — abstracted and simplified to the point of being a gentle geometric form, not a realistic illustration — holding or cradling a small leaf. The hand is the organization (care, support, protection). The leaf is the life being spoken for.

**Geometry:**

- The hand is NOT a realistic illustration. It is an abstracted, simplified form — approximately 4–5 smooth curves forming the palm and finger silhouette in a single, unified shape with no internal detail
- The fingers are not individually articulated — the hand reads as a soft, open, cupped form, like a nest or a bowl
- The leaf rests in the center of the cupped palm — the same leaf form from Concept A, smaller, centered
- The overall mark is round or squarish — fits within a 1:1.1 ratio bounding box

**Important caution for this concept:**
The hand must be so abstracted that it reads first as a shelter or cup, with the "hand" interpretation arriving as a secondary reading. If the hand reads as literal or realistic, it risks feeling sentimental or clinical. If uncertain, use Concept A instead.

On all four sides of every logo version, maintain a minimum clear space equal to **the cap height of the letter "V" in "Voices"** in that version. No text, imagery, or other elements may enter this zone.

```
      ↑ [cap height of V]
← [V] [ Mark ][ Wordmark ] [V] →
      ↓ [cap height of V]
```

## Part 7 — Absolute Prohibition Rules

| Prohibited                                                                          | Why                                                             |
| ----------------------------------------------------------------------------------- | --------------------------------------------------------------- |
| Stretching or distorting the mark or wordmark proportions                           | Destroys brand integrity                                        |
| Placing any logo on a background where contrast is below 4.5:1                      | Accessibility failure                                           |
| Drop shadows, glows, bevels, or emboss effects on any logo element                  | Feels dated, undermines credibility                             |
| Using any color not in the brand palette                                            | Brand consistency                                               |
| Rotating any logo version at any angle                                              | Brand integrity                                                 |
| Placing the mark inside a containing shape (badge, circle, square) without approval | Changes visual context                                          |
| Rasterizing the wordmark letterforms with an outline stroke                         | Reduces legibility                                              |
| Using red, anywhere, for any reason                                                 | Red carries alarming and political associations on this subject |
| Using pure black (`#000000`)                                                        | Too harsh — use Charcoal Navy instead                           |
| Adding gradients to the wordmark                                                    | Legibility and reproduction failure                             |
| Using any font other than Merriweather Bold for the organization name               | Brand integrity                                                 |
| Depicting fetuses, embryos, or any clinical medical imagery                         | Wrong for this brand — alienates the audience                   |
| Including any explicitly religious symbols (cross, crucifix, etc.)                  | Non-denominational default                                      |
| Using baby imagery or baby footprints                                               | Clichéd for this subject matter; risks political coding         |
| Using hands in political gesture (raised fist, pointing, etc.)                      | Wrong emotional register for the brand                          |

## Part 9 — Style Consistency Checklist

Before delivering any asset, verify against every item:

**Visual language:**

- [ ] Organic, curved forms throughout — no sharp, aggressive angles
- [ ] Strokes are minimum 1.5pt equivalent — not hairline thin
- [ ] No more than 4 distinct visual elements in any mark or logo
- [ ] The icon mark is readable at 32×32px without losing identity
- [ ] The mark carries the ideas of Voice, Hope, and Life — even if subtly

**Color:**

- [ ] Only brand palette colors used (see Part 2)
- [ ] No red anywhere in any asset
- [ ] No pure black (`#000000`) — Charcoal Navy (`#26313C`) always
- [ ] Primary logo contrast ratio is 4.5:1 or higher against its intended background
- [ ] Warm Apricot (`#F4A261`) used sparingly — accent only, not dominant

**Tone:**

- [ ] The asset makes the viewer feel safe and welcomed — not alarmed
- [ ] No clinical, medical, or procedural visual references
- [ ] Nothing that reads as a political campaign or protest visual
- [ ] No explicitly religious symbolism
- [ ] No baby, infant, or fetus imagery in any form
- [ ] No hands in political gesture
- [ ] No graphic imagery of any kind

**Craft:**

- [ ] SVG files are clean, optimized, and have no stray anchor points
- [ ] The mark works in both full color and monochrome
- [ ] All logo versions are optically balanced — not just mathematically scaled
- [ ] Clear space rules have been observed in all composed versions
- [ ] The wordmark is set in Merriweather Bold — verified

## Part 12 — The Name as a Design Compass

Every time you are uncertain about a design decision, return to the name:

> **Voices Bringing Hope to Life**

Ask: Does this visual carry a sense of **voice** — something being spoken, expressed, rising?
Ask: Does this visual carry **hope** — warmth, light, upward movement, possibility?
Ask: Does this visual carry **life** — growth, nature, something living and becoming?

If the answer to all three is yes — even subtly — the design is right.
If any answer is no — simplify, soften, or reconsider.

The slogan adds one final test:

> **Because Every Life Deserves a Voice.**

Ask: Does this design make the visitor feel that _she_ is being seen, heard, and spoken for?

If yes — you have found the mark.
